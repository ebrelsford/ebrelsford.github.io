Part 2: Geoprocessing

Geoprocessing tools give you the means to overlay multiple vector layers in order to modify the layers and potentially combine them. You are most likely going to find the following geoprocessing functions to be the most useful:

  • clipping
  • buffers
  • difference

You may find a use for the other items under Vector > Geoprocessing Tools in QGIS. Experiment with a few datasets and at least two geoprocessing functions. Take screenshots of the results that you get and briefly write about them in your response. What are they doing? How might they be useful? Note that you do not have to make a print composer for this part of the assignment.

Part 3: Joins

When you join two or more datasets, you combine them feature-by-feature, and this often gives attributes to a feature that it would not have otherwise had. There are two types of joins: spatial and attribute.

Spatial joins add attributes to features based on where they are spatially. For example, if you have parks and city council districts and want the parks to have some of the attributes from their corresponding council districts, you would use a spatial join to do this. In QGIS, you do this by going to Vector > Data Management Tools > Join Attributes by Location. Use a spatial join to add data to features and submit two screenshots: (1) the attribute table of the features before the join and (2) the attribute table of the features after the join.

Attribute joins add attributes to features based on a unique identifier. Often you will have a spatial dataset (such as a shapefile) and a CSV file with extra data that you would like to map. As long as both the spatial data and the CSV have a common unique identifier (sometimes this is a name, but it could be a unique number or an address) you can join them in QGIS and add the data from the CSV to the spatial data. Combine two datasets using an attribute join and submit two screenshots: (1) the attribute table of the features before the join, and (2) the attribute table of the features after the join. If you're having trouble finding suitable datasets, consider joining the BBLs (aka "borough, block, and lot numbers") in this oil boilers set with those in MapPLUTO for one borough (or more if you like), or getting data through the Census Bureau's American FactFinder. If you'd like more of a challenge, create BBLs for the rows in the ECB's notice of violations and join those with a borough in MapPLUTO. There is no penalty for using these datasets, this is purely an opportunity to work with this operation on your own.
